# SKILL 09: Citation Mastery

> **Citations are the currency of academia. Master their strategic deployment to position your work within the scholarly conversation and maximize its impact.**

## COMPONENT 1: CITATION STRATEGY FRAMEWORK

### The Four Citation Tiers

Not all citations are equal. Each reference in your bibliography serves a specific strategic purpose. Classify every citation into one of four tiers:

#### TIER 1: Foundational Citations (15–20% of references)

**Definition**: Seminal works that established the theoretical, methodological, or empirical foundation upon which your research builds.

**Purpose**: Position your work within the established intellectual tradition; demonstrate mastery of the field's core literature.

**Selection criteria**:
- Works that defined the research area (the "origin story" papers)
- Highly cited foundational papers (typically 500+ citations)
- Papers that introduced key concepts, frameworks, or terminology still in use
- Classic methodological papers that established standard approaches

**Examples**:
- In machine learning: Rumelhart et al. (1986) on backpropagation; Vaswani et al. (2017) on Transformers
- In psychology: Kahneman & Tversky (1979) on Prospect Theory; Bandura (1977) on Social Learning Theory
- In medicine: Flexner (1910) on medical education reform; Cochrane (1972) on evidence-based medicine

**Placement**: Primarily in Introduction and Theoretical Framework sections; occasionally in Methods for foundational methodological references.

---

#### TIER 2: Engagement Citations (40–50% of references)

**Definition**: Recent and ongoing work by active researchers in your specific niche — the papers your paper is in direct conversation with.

**Purpose**: Demonstrate that you are current with the literature; position your work relative to the most relevant recent findings; show the "conversation" your paper joins.

**Selection criteria**:
- Papers published in the last 3–5 years on the same or closely related topics
- Papers by researchers whose work directly intersects with yours
- Papers that your findings confirm, contradict, or extend
- Papers from the target journal (signals familiarity with the venue)

**Examples**:
- "Building on the recent work of Smith et al. (2023), who demonstrated..."
- "Unlike Johnson & Lee (2022), who found X, we observe Y under condition Z..."
- "Our approach extends the framework proposed by Garcia et al. (2024) by..."

**Placement**: Throughout the manuscript, especially in Introduction (gap identification), Results (comparison with prior findings), and Discussion (contextualizing results).

---

#### TIER 3: Methodological Citations (20–25% of references)

**Definition**: Papers that provide the methodological tools, analytical techniques, or experimental protocols used in your study.

**Purpose**: Justify your methodological choices; enable readers to understand and evaluate your approach; provide provenance for analytical tools.

**Selection criteria**:
- Original papers describing the methods or tools you used
- Validation studies demonstrating the reliability of your measures
- Statistical software or package citations
- Protocol papers for standardized procedures
- Benchmark or dataset papers for evaluation resources

**Examples**:
- "We used the PHQ-9 (Kroenke et al., 2001) to assess depression severity"
- "Analyses were conducted using lme4 (Bates et al., 2015) in R (R Core Team, 2024)"
- "We followed the PRISMA guidelines (Page et al., 2021) for our systematic review"

**Placement**: Primarily in Methods section; some in Results (for analytical approach citations) and Supplementary Materials.

---

#### TIER 4: Strategic Citations (10–15% of references)

**Definition**: Citations included for strategic rather than purely intellectual reasons — to build goodwill, signal community membership, or increase citation visibility.

**Purpose**: Increase the likelihood of favorable review and citation by key community members; acknowledge the broader ecosystem; meet journal expectations.

**Selection criteria**:
- Recent work by likely reviewers (identifiable through editorial board analysis)
- Recent work by the target journal's editorial team
- Work by researchers in your extended network who might cite you back
- Papers from the target journal's recent issues
- Papers that represent perspectives you want to acknowledge even if tangential

**Examples**:
- Citing an associate editor's recent work (when genuinely relevant)
- Including a paper from the target journal's most recent issue on a related topic
- Citing work from a research group you want to engage with for future collaboration

**Important ethical note**: Strategic citations must be genuinely relevant to your work. Never cite irrelevant papers purely to appease editors or reviewers. This constitutes citation manipulation and is an ethical violation.

**Placement**: Distributed throughout the manuscript where contextually appropriate.

---

### Citation Tier Distribution Guidelines

| Tier | Target % | Range | Too Few Signals | Too Many Signals |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Foundational | 15–20% | 10–25% | Unaware of field history | Stuck in the past |
| Engagement | 40–50% | 35–55% | Out of touch with current work | Following trends uncritically |
| Methodological | 20–25% | 15–30% | Insufficient methodological grounding | Method-heavy, contribution-light |
| Strategic | 10–15% | 5–20% | Missing community connections | Citation manipulation |

## COMPONENT 2: CITATION QUALITY RULES

### What TO Do

1. **Cite the original source**, not a secondary citation. If Paper A cites Paper B for a finding, read Paper B and cite it directly.

2. **Cite the specific finding**, not the entire paper. Be precise about what claim you're attributing: "Smith et al. (2023) found that X increases Y by 23% under condition Z" — not "Smith et al. (2023) found interesting results."

3. **Cite recent work alongside foundational work**. A literature review that stops at 2018 signals that you haven't kept up. Include foundational papers AND the last 2–3 years of active research.

4. **Cite contradictory evidence**. Acknowledging opposing findings demonstrates intellectual honesty and strengthens your position by showing you've considered alternatives.

5. **Cite work from diverse sources**. Avoid citing only from one research group, one country, or one journal. Diversity signals thoroughness.

6. **Cite with purpose**. Every citation should serve one of: establishing foundation, identifying a gap, supporting a claim, providing method, or acknowledging context. If a citation serves none of these, remove it.

7. **Cite the version you actually read**. If you read a preprint, cite the preprint. If the paper is now published, cite the published version. Never cite a paper you haven't read.

8. **Update citations before submission**. Check that preprints have been published; verify that working papers have been finalized; update to the most recent version.

9. **Use primary sources for methodological citations**. Cite the paper that introduced the method, not a paper that used it. Cite the software package itself, not just a paper that mentions it.

10. **Ensure citation accuracy**. Verify author names, year, journal, volume, pages. Incorrect citations erode credibility.

### What NOT to Do

1. **❌ Don't over-cite yourself**. Self-citation rates above 15–20% raise red flags. Most fields consider 5–10% self-citation normal.

2. **❌ Don't cite without reading**. Citing a paper based on its abstract or another author's description of it is intellectual dishonesty.

3. **❌ Don't cherry-pick citations**. Selectively citing only supportive evidence while ignoring contradictory studies is a form of confirmation bias.

4. **❌ Don't citation-bomb**. Packing a sentence with 8+ citations ("Many studies have found X (A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H)") is uninformative. Be selective and explain the relevance.

5. **❌ Don't cite obsolete findings**. If a finding has been superseded, cite the current understanding and note the evolution. Don't present outdated results as current.

6. **❌ Don't engage in coercive citation**. Never add citations simply because a reviewer demands you cite their work, unless the citation is genuinely relevant.

7. **❌ Don't cite retracted papers** without noting the retraction. Always check retraction databases before citing.

8. **❌ Don't use citations as adjectives**. "The Smith et al. method" is appropriate; "The Smith et al. (2023) important method" is not — let the work speak for itself.

9. **❌ Don't cite predatory journals**. Publications in predatory journals lack peer review and may contain unreliable findings. Verify journal quality before citing.

10. **❌ Don't ignore non-English literature** when relevant. If important work exists in other languages, acknowledge it (and note language barriers in your limitations if appropriate).

## COMPONENT 3: FORMAT ENGINE

Auto-format citations and references for the five most common academic styles.

### Quick Reference: In-Text Citation Format

| Style | Single Author | Two Authors | 3+ Authors | Group Author |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **APA 7th** | (Smith, 2023) | (Smith & Jones, 2023) | (Smith et al., 2023) | (WHO, 2023) |
| **IEEE** | [1] | [1] | [1] | [1] |
| **Nature** | (Smith, 2023) | (Smith & Jones, 2023) | (Smith et al., 2023) | (WHO, 2023) |
| **AMA** | Smith1 | Smith and Jones1,2 | Smith et al1 | WHO1 |
| **Vancouver** | Smith(1) | Smith and Jones(1,2) | Smith et al(1) | WHO(1) |

### Quick Reference: Reference List Format — Journal Article

**APA 7th:**
```
Smith, J. A., & Jones, B. C. (2023). Title of the article in lowercase.
Journal Name in Title Case, 45(3), 123–145. https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxx
```

**IEEE:**
```
J. A. Smith and B. C. Jones, "Title of the article in quotation marks,"
Journal Name in Title Case, vol. 45, no. 3, pp. 123–145, Mar. 2023,
doi: 10.xxxx/xxxx.
```

**Nature:**
```
Smith, J. A. & Jones, B. C. Title of the article in sentence case.
J. Name 45, 123–145 (2023).
```

**AMA:**
```
Smith JA, Jones BC. Title of the article in sentence case. J Name.
2023;45(3):123-145. doi:10.xxxx/xxxx
```

**Vancouver:**
```
Smith JA, Jones BC. Title of the article in sentence case. J Name.
2023;45(3):123-145. doi:10.xxxx/xxxx
```

### Quick Reference: Reference List Format — Book

**APA 7th:**
```
Smith, J. A. (2023). Title of the book in sentence case (2nd ed.).
Publisher. https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxx
```

**IEEE:**
```
J. A. Smith, Title of the Book in Title Case, 2nd ed. City, Country:
Publisher, 2023.
```

**Nature:**
```
Smith, J. A. Title of the Book in Sentence Case 2nd edn (Publisher, 2023).
```

**AMA:**
```
Smith JA. Title of the Book in Sentence Case. 2nd ed. Publisher;
2023.
```

**Vancouver:**
```
Smith JA. Title of the Book in Sentence Case. 2nd ed. City: Publisher;
2023.
```

### Quick Reference: Reference List Format — Conference Paper

**APA 7th:**
```
Smith, J. A. (2023). Title of the paper. In B. C. Jones (Ed.),
Proceedings of the Conference Name (pp. 123–130). Publisher.
https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxx
```

**IEEE:**
```
J. A. Smith, "Title of the paper," in Proc. Conference Name, City,
Country, 2023, pp. 123–130, doi: 10.xxxx/xxxx.
```

**Nature:**
```
Smith, J. A. in Proceedings of the Conference Name (ed Jones, B. C.)
123–130 (Publisher, 2023).
```

### Quick Reference: Reference List Format — Online/Dataset

**APA 7th (Online):**
```
Smith, J. A. (2023, March 15). Title of the webpage. Website Name.
https://url.com
```

**APA 7th (Dataset):**
```
Smith, J. A. (2023). Dataset title [Data set]. Repository.
https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxx
```

**IEEE (Online):**
```
J. A. Smith, "Title of the webpage," Website Name. [Online].
Available: https://url.com. [Accessed: Mar. 15, 2023].
```
