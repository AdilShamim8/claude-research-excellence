# Novelty Stress Testing Framework

## Purpose
Every paper claims to be novel. Most aren't as novel as their authors believe. This framework provides a rigorous methodology for stress-testing novelty claims before submission — because if you don't test your novelty, reviewers will, and they will be less charitable.

---

## 1. The "So What" Test for Novelty Claims

### The Three-Level Test

**Level 1: The Stranger Test**
Tell a stranger in your field (not your subfield) what you found. If they say "So what?" or "Didn't we already know that?" — your novelty claim fails.

**Level 2: The Competitor Test**
Imagine your strongest competitor reads this paper. Would they feel threatened, or would they think "We already showed something similar"?

**Level 3: The Reviewer Test**
A reviewer at your target journal has read 200 papers this year on this topic. What makes yours memorable? If you can't answer in one sentence, the novelty is insufficient.

### The Novelty Statement Test

Write a single sentence: "This paper is novel because [specific reason]."

Check your sentence against these criteria:

| Criterion | Pass | Fail |
|---|---|---|
| Specificity | Names a specific mechanism, boundary, or method | Says "advances understanding" or "contributes to the literature" |
| Verifiability | Could be confirmed or falsified | Is unfalsifiable ("provides new insights") |
| Incremental distance | Clearly beyond the closest prior work | Could be described as a replication with minor variation |
| Audience value | Would change how someone works | Would not change anyone's behavior |
| Temporal durability | Will remain novel for 2+ years | Will be superseded within months |

## 2. Competitive Landscape Analysis

### Step 1: Identify the Competitors

List the 5-10 closest papers to yours. For each, answer:

| Question | Paper 1 | Paper 2 | Paper 3 | ... |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What did they find? | | | | |
| What is the overlap with your study? | | | | |
| What did they NOT find that you do? | | | | |
| How recent is it? | | | | |
| How visible is it? (Journal impact) | | | | |

### Step 2: The Differentiation Matrix

| Dimension | Your Paper | Closest Competitor 1 | Closest Competitor 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Research question | | | |
| Sample | | | |
| Method | | | |
| Key finding | | | |
| Mechanism | | | |
| Boundary condition | | | |
| Practical implication | | | |

**Rule**: If your paper differs from the closest competitor on only ONE dimension (typically sample), your novelty is incremental at best.

### Step 3: The Who-Else Check

Search for researchers who are likely working on the same question:
1. Who has published on this topic in the last 3 years?
2. Who has presented at the last 2 conferences on this topic?
3. Who has preprints on this topic?
4. Who has grants funded on this topic?

If 5+ active research groups are working on the same question, you are in a competitive space. Speed matters, and your novelty claim must be razor-sharp.

### Step 4: The Preprint Scan

Search bioRxiv, psyarXiv, SSRN, and other preprint servers for:
- Your exact research question
- Your specific method
- Your target population
- Your predicted mechanism

If a preprint already addresses your question, your novelty window may be closing. Act accordingly.

## 3. Novelty Degradation Over Time

### The Novelty Half-Life

Novelty degrades over time as the field catches up. Estimate the half-life of your novelty claim:

| Research Type | Novelty Half-Life | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Hot topic (many groups working on it) | 6-12 months | Rapid progress; preprints appear frequently |
| Active area (some groups working on it) | 1-2 years | Steady progress; conference presentations precede publications |
| Established area (few groups, slow progress) | 3-5 years | Infrequent new findings; novelty persists |
| Niche area (very few groups) | 5+ years | Limited competition; novelty is rarely challenged |

### The Novelty Degradation Formula

```
Remaining Novelty = Initial Novelty × (0.5)^(Time / Half-Life)
```

If your study took 2 years from design to submission, and the half-life is 12 months, your novelty has degraded to 25% of its initial value. This means:
- Your novelty claim must have been very strong to begin with
- You must highlight what makes the work novel NOW, not what made it novel when you started

### Signs Your Novelty Is Eroding

1. Papers published while you were collecting data address the same question
2. Conference presentations report similar findings
3. Reviewers in your department mention "someone just published something like this"
4. Your introduction's gap paragraph now cites papers that fill the gap you identified
5. A meta-analysis now exists that would have made your study unnecessary

## 4. Incremental vs Substantial vs Paradigm-Shifting Classification

### The Three Tiers of Novelty

#### Tier 1: Incremental (Most Papers)

**Definition**: Extends existing findings by a small step — new sample, new context, minor methodological variation.

**Characteristics**:
- Adds a new moderator to an established effect
- Replicates an effect in a different population
- Uses a slightly improved measure
- Tests one additional hypothesis in a well-studied paradigm
- Confirms a finding with a larger sample

**Legitimate claim language**: "extends prior findings by..." / "replicates X in Y context" / "adds boundary condition Z"

**Target journals**: Field-specific journals; specialty journals; PLOS ONE; Frontiers

**Honest self-assessment**: ~70% of published papers are incremental. This is fine — science advances incrementally. The problem is claiming incremental work is substantial.

#### Tier 2: Substantial (Strong Papers)

**Definition**: Makes a meaningful advance — identifies a mechanism, resolves a debate, establishes a boundary condition that changes interpretation, or introduces a method that enables new questions.

**Characteristics**:
- Identifies the mechanism behind an established effect
- Resolves a debate between competing theoretical accounts
- Shows that an established finding does NOT hold under specific conditions
- Introduces a paradigm that enables testing previously untestable questions
- Provides the first evidence in a domain where none existed
- Demonstrates that a widely-accepted finding is an artifact

**Legitimate claim language**: "identifies the mechanism by which..." / "resolves the debate between X and Y" / "challenges the assumption that..."

**Target journals**: Top field journals; interdisciplinary journals; Journal of Personality and Social Psychology; Psychological Science

**Honest self-assessment**: ~25% of published papers are substantial. These are the papers that get cited regularly for 5+ years.

#### Tier 3: Paradigm-Shifting (Rare)

**Definition**: Fundamentally changes how the field thinks about a problem — creates a new framework, discovers a phenomenon that existing theory cannot explain, or develops a method that opens a new field.

**Characteristics**:
- Proposes a framework that reframes existing findings
- Discovers a phenomenon that contradicts the dominant theoretical account
- Develops a method that enables investigation of previously inaccessible questions
- Shows that an entire research program rests on a flawed assumption
- Creates a new field or subfield

**Legitimate claim language**: "challenges the foundational assumption that..." / "provides an alternative framework for understanding..."

**Target journals**: Science; Nature; Psychological Review; Behavioral and Brain Sciences

**Honest self-assessment**: ~5% of published papers are paradigm-shifting. Most papers that claim to be paradigm-shifting are not. If you have to argue that your paper is paradigm-shifting, it probably isn't.

### Self-Classification Test

Answer these questions honestly:

1. Could your finding be predicted by a straightforward extension of existing theory? → Incremental
2. Does your finding surprise someone who knows the literature well? → Substantial
3. Does your finding make a significant portion of the existing literature need reinterpretation? → Paradigm-shifting

Most papers that authors classify as Tier 2 are actually Tier 1. Most papers that authors classify as Tier 3 are actually Tier 2. Downgrade by one tier as your default.

## 5. Novelty Defense Strategies

### Strategy 1: The Mechanism Defense
"I don't just show THAT X happens; I show WHY it happens (the mechanism)."

This is the strongest novelty defense. Knowing the mechanism changes what interventions are possible. It converts "X occurs" into "X occurs because of M, so targeting M will change X."

**Requirements**: Must include mediation analysis or experimental manipulation of the mechanism.

### Strategy 2: The Boundary Defense
"I don't just show THAT X happens; I show WHEN it happens and when it doesn't (the boundary condition)."

Boundary conditions are scientifically valuable because they constrain theory. An effect that holds universally is less informative than one that holds only under specific conditions.

**Requirements**: Must include moderation analysis or experimental manipulation of the boundary.

### Strategy 3: The Reconciliation Defense
"I don't just show X; I reconcile the apparent contradiction between Study A (X) and Study B (not-X)."

Reconciliation papers are highly valued because they advance theory by explaining why the field has been confused.

**Requirements**: Must test a condition that explains when X holds and when it doesn't, accounting for the difference between prior studies.

### Strategy 4: The Method Defense
"I don't just show X; I show it with a method that rules out the confounds that plagued prior work."

Methodological innovation enables stronger causal claims or access to previously unmeasurable constructs.

**Requirements**: Must demonstrate that the new method produces different (more valid) results than the old method, or enables tests that were previously impossible.

### Strategy 5: The Null-Result Defense
"I show that X does NOT happen, contrary to widely-held belief."

Well-powered null results that challenge established effects are highly valuable, especially in the current replication crisis context.

**Requirements**: Must demonstrate adequate power to detect the effect; must provide Bayesian evidence or equivalence testing; must show that the prior positive result was likely a false positive.

## 6. Pre-Submission Novelty Audit Checklist

### Before You Submit, Answer Every Question

**The Gap Test**:
- [ ] Is the gap stated explicitly (not implied)?
- [ ] Is the gap specific enough to be filled by one study?
- [ ] Is the gap important enough that filling it matters?
- [ ] Has someone already filled this gap? (Search thoroughly.)
- [ ] Could the gap be filled by re-analyzing existing data?

**The Contribution Test**:
- [ ] Can you state the contribution in one sentence?
- [ ] Does the contribution go beyond "we found the same thing in a different sample"?
- [ ] Would the contribution survive if someone published a similar result tomorrow?
- [ ] Is the contribution verifiable (can someone check if you're right)?

**The Competitor Test**:
- [ ] Have you searched for active competitors in this space?
- [ ] Have you checked preprint servers for recent uploads?
- [ ] Have you checked conference programs for upcoming presentations?
- [ ] Is your differentiation from the closest prior work clear and defensible?

**The Durability Test**:
- [ ] How long will this contribution remain novel?
- [ ] Is the novelty half-life shorter than the review process? (If yes, consider a faster journal.)
- [ ] Are there technological or theoretical developments that will make this obsolete soon?

**The Language Test**:
- [ ] Does the novelty claim language match the actual novelty level?
- [ ] Have you avoided "novel," "first," "groundbreaking," and "paradigm-shifting"?
- [ ] Would a skeptical reviewer accept your novelty claim?

## 7. How to Reframe Incremental Work to Highlight Genuine Contribution

### Reframing Principle
You cannot make incremental work substantial. But you CAN make incremental work's genuine contribution visible. The key is to identify what is genuinely new — even if small — and present it clearly.

### Reframing Technique 1: Mechanism Shift
Instead of: "We replicate X in a new sample"
Reframe as: "We identify the mechanism that accounts for X — a mechanism that was previously assumed but never tested"

If you tested and confirmed a mechanism, that IS the contribution, not the replication.

### Reframing Technique 2: Boundary Specification
Instead of: "We find X in context Y"
Reframe as: "We establish the boundary condition for X: it holds in Y but not in Z, which constrains the theoretical explanation to [specific mechanism]"

Boundary conditions are more valuable than replications because they constrain theory.

### Reframing Technique 3: Debunking
Instead of: "We fail to replicate X"
Reframe as: "We provide evidence that X may not be a robust phenomenon, using a well-powered design with [N] participants — 4× the original sample — and [methodological improvement that addresses the original study's limitations]"

Well-powered failures to replicate are contributions, especially of highly-cited effects.

### Reframing Technique 4: Integration
Instead of: "We test X"
Reframe as: "We integrate two literatures that have developed independently — [Literature A] and [Literature B] — by testing whether [phenomenon from A] operates through [mechanism from B]"

Integration papers that connect previously separate domains are valued because they unify knowledge.

### Reframing Technique 5: Methodological Enablement
Instead of: "We developed a new measure"
Reframe as: "We develop and validate a measure that enables [specific new research question] that was previously untestable because [what was missing]"

The contribution is not the measure itself but the research it enables.

### Anti-Patterns in Reframing

❌ **Inflating**: "Our study of 50 undergraduates revolutionizes the understanding of social cognition" (Overclaiming)
❌ **Misdirection**: Drawing attention to a minor methodological innovation to distract from the thinness of the conceptual contribution
❌ **Comparison distortion**: Comparing to the weakest prior study instead of the strongest
❌ **Vague novelty**: "This study contributes to the growing literature on X" (Every study does this)

## 8. When to Pivot: Signs Your Novelty Claim Won't Hold

### The 10 Warning Signs

1. **Your gap paragraph cites more than 3 papers from the last 2 years** — the gap is closing fast.

2. **You can't state the contribution without using "also"** — "We ALSO found that..." suggests it's an addition, not a discovery.

3. **Your closest comparator differs on only one dimension** — same paradigm, same measures, same analysis, different sample.

4. **A reviewer at a conference asked "How is this different from [study you hadn't heard of]?"** — your literature search was incomplete.

5. **Your novelty claim is in the method, not the finding** — methodological novelty requires demonstrating that the method produces insights unavailable with prior methods.

6. **You find yourself writing "While previous studies have examined X, none have examined X in [specific context]"** — this is the weakest form of novelty (context extension).

7. **The effect size you found is smaller than the original study** — you may be publishing a diminished replication dressed as a novel contribution.

8. **You chose the journal before the finding** — targeting a lower-tier journal because the finding is incremental is honest; targeting a high-tier journal by inflating the novelty claim is dishonest.

9. **Your pre-registration and your paper tell different stories** — if you changed the framing after seeing the data, the novelty was data-driven, not theory-driven.

10. **You can't explain the contribution to a non-expert in under 30 seconds** — if the novelty requires jargon and caveats to articulate, it may not be substantial.

### The Pivot Decision Framework

| Sign Count | Recommendation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 0-1 | Proceed with confidence | Submit to target journal |
| 2-3 | Strengthen the novelty claim | Apply reframing techniques; add mechanism test; tighten the gap |
| 4-5 | Significant revision needed | Consider additional study or analysis; lower target journal tier |
| 6-7 | Fundamental novelty problem | Redesign the study to test a more novel question; or pivot to a different angle |
| 8-10 | The novelty claim won't hold | Either (a) accept this as incremental work and target an appropriate journal, or (b) fundamentally redesign the research program |

### When Pivoting Is the Right Call

Pivoting is not failure — it is strategic adaptation. The best researchers pivot when evidence tells them their initial novelty claim is weak. Consider pivoting when:

1. **A competitor published your finding first** — pivot to a mechanism or boundary test they didn't conduct
2. **The effect is much smaller than expected** — pivot from "X is a major phenomenon" to "X's boundary conditions are narrow, informing theory about why"
3. **The design has a confound you can't fix post-hoc** — pivot from a causal claim to an associative claim, or add a second study
4. **The literature has moved on** — pivot to address the current state of the debate, not the state when you designed the study

The key question is: "Given what I know NOW, what is the most valuable contribution this work can make?" That contribution may be different from what you originally intended, and that is fine — as long as you are honest about it.
