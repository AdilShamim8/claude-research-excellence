# Introduction Crafting Guide

## Purpose
The introduction is the paper's argument for existence. It must convince the reader that a gap exists, that the gap matters, and that this study fills it. This guide provides a paragraph-by-paragraph architecture for introductions that are impossible to stop reading.

---

## 1. The Hourglass Structure in Detail

The introduction follows an hourglass: broad → narrow → specific → your study.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════╗
║  PARAGRAPH 1: THE FIELD (broad) ║  ← Why the domain matters
╠══════════════════════════════════╣
║  PARAGRAPH 2: THE PROGRESS      ║  ← What is known (narrowing)
╠══════════════════════════════════╣
║  PARAGRAPH 3: THE GAP (narrow)  ║  ← What is NOT known
╠══════════════════════════════════╣
║  PARAGRAPH 4: ALTERNATIVES      ║  ← Competing explanations (optional)
╠══════════════════════════════════╣
║  PARAGRAPH 5: THIS STUDY        ║  ← What you did and predicted
╚══════════════════════════════════╝
```

The width of the hourglass represents the scope of claims. Paragraph 1 makes the broadest claims. Paragraph 3 makes the most specific. Paragraph 5 broadens slightly as you state the study's purpose.

### Why This Structure Works
1. **Paragraph 1** gives the reader a reason to care (relevance)
2. **Paragraph 2** establishes your credibility (you know the literature)
3. **Paragraph 3** creates tension (the gap is real and important)
4. **Paragraph 4** (optional) shows sophistication (you considered alternatives)
5. **Paragraph 5** resolves the tension (your study fills the gap)

## 2. Paragraph-by-Paragraph Templates with Examples

### PARAGRAPH 1: THE FIELD (4-6 sentences)

**Purpose**: Establish that the broad domain matters. Make someone outside your subfield care.

**Sentence-level template**:
1. Opening claim: "[Broad phenomenon] is [fundamental/essential/critical] to [larger domain]."
2. Elaboration: "Specifically, [more specific claim about the phenomenon's importance]."
3. Consequence: "When [phenomenon] [fails/is disrupted/operates suboptimally], [real-world consequence]."
4. Scope statement: "Understanding [specific aspect] is therefore [important/necessary] for [practical/theoretical reason]."
5. Transition: "Central to this understanding is [the concept/variable you study]."

**Full example (Social Psychology — Trust)**:

"Trust is the foundation of cooperative social systems, enabling economic exchange, political governance, and interpersonal relationships (Coleman, 1990; Fukuyama, 1995; Ostrom & Walker, 2003). When trust erodes, coordination failures proliferate: markets freeze, institutions lose legitimacy, and communities fragment (Putnam, 2000; Rothstein & Uslaner, 2005). Recent economic instability has intensified concerns about trust decline, with survey data from 42 countries showing a 15-percentage-point drop in interpersonal trust over the past two decades (OECD, 2022). Understanding the mechanisms that sustain or undermine trust is therefore critical for designing institutions that promote cooperation. Central to this understanding is the role of uncertainty: trust is fundamentally a bet on another's future behavior under conditions of incomplete information."

### PARAGRAPH 2: THE PROGRESS (5-7 sentences)

**Purpose**: Show what is known. Synthesize, don't list. Group by theme, not by author.

**Sentence-level template**:
1. Theme statement: "Research over the past [timeframe] has established [what is known at a high level]."
2. Theme 1: "Specifically, [finding cluster 1]: [studies] have shown that [synthesized finding]."
3. Theme 2 (if applicable): "Additionally, [finding cluster 2]: [synthesized finding from second theme]."
4. Nuance: "However, [subtlety or boundary condition in existing findings]."
5. Convergence: "Despite different [methods/approaches], these findings converge on [core established principle]."
6. Boundary: "What remains at the boundary of this knowledge is [the frontier where the gap lives]."

**Full example (continued)**:

"Research over the past two decades has established that trust decisions are shaped by both dispositional and situational factors. At the dispositional level, generalized trust — the tendency to assume others are trustworthy — predicts cooperative behavior across contexts (Berg et al., 1995; Evans & Krueger, 2009; Johnson & Mislin, 2011). At the situational level, trust increases when partners have reputational stakes (Barclay, 2004), when communication is possible (Bicchieri & Lev-On, 2007), and when institutional safeguards exist (Kosfeld et al., 2005). Importantly, these factors interact: dispositional trust magnifies sensitivity to situational cues, such that high-trusters respond more strongly to reputation signals than low-trusters (Delgado et al., 2005). Despite differences in method — from behavioral games to neuroimaging to field studies — these findings converge on a core principle: trust is not a fixed trait but a context-sensitive adaptation. What remains at the boundary of this knowledge is how trust adapts when uncertainty is not merely incomplete information but is actively ambiguous — where the very rules of the interaction are unclear."

### PARAGRAPH 3: THE GAP (3-5 sentences)

**Purpose**: Name the gap explicitly. Explain why it exists. Explain why filling it matters.

**Sentence-level template**:
1. Gap statement: "What remains unknown is [specific gap]."
2. Why the gap exists: "This gap persists because [methodological/theoretical reason prior work couldn't address it]."
3. Why it matters: "Without understanding [the gap content], [theoretical/practical consequence of not knowing]."
4. Distinguish from other gaps: "This is distinct from [related but different gap], which [has already been addressed / is beyond the scope of this study]."
5. Implication: "Resolving this question would [specific advance]."

**Full example (continued)**:

"What remains unknown is how trust decisions adapt under conditions of radical uncertainty — where the probability of reciprocity, the payoff structure, and even the number of interaction partners are unknown. This gap persists because existing trust paradigms (e.g., the Trust Game, Public Goods Game) fix these parameters, making them powerful for studying trust under known risk but inadequate for studying trust under ambiguity. Without understanding how trust operates under ambiguity, we cannot explain why trust breaks down in complex real-world crises (e.g., pandemics, financial crashes) where parameters are precisely what is unknown. This is distinct from the question of how trust generalizes across known contexts, which has been addressed by recent cross-situational studies (McEvily et al., 2023). Resolving this question would extend trust theory from risk-based to ambiguity-based frameworks, a shift with implications for institutional design under crisis conditions."

### PARAGRAPH 4: ALTERNATIVE ACCOUNTS (3-4 sentences, optional)

**Purpose**: Acknowledge competing explanations and preview how your design distinguishes them.

**Sentence-level template**:
1. "Two accounts could explain [gap phenomenon]: [Account A] and [Account B]."
2. "Account A predicts [specific prediction A], whereas Account B predicts [specific prediction B]."
3. "These predictions diverge under [specific condition], providing an empirical test."
4. "The present study exploits this divergence by [design feature that creates the distinguishing condition]."

**Full example (continued)**:

"Two accounts could explain trust under ambiguity: the uncertainty-avoidance account, which predicts that ambiguity reduces trust through risk aversion (Ellsberg, 1961), and the complexity-override account, which predicts that ambiguity triggers heuristic processing that defaults to dispositional trust levels (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974). The uncertainty-avoidance account predicts uniformly lower trust under ambiguity, whereas the complexity-override account predicts that high-trusters maintain trust while low-trusters further reduce it, amplifying dispositional differences. These predictions diverge when comparing high- and low-trusters under ambiguous versus risky conditions, providing a direct test. The present study exploits this divergence by orthogonally manipulating uncertainty type (risk vs. ambiguity) and measuring dispositional trust as an individual difference."

### PARAGRAPH 5: THE PRESENT STUDY (4-6 sentences)

**Purpose**: State what you did, why, and what you predicted. The study fills the gap.

**Sentence-level template**:
1. Study description: "In the present study, we [method/design summary] to test [the gap question]."
2. Hypothesis 1: "We hypothesized that [H1 prediction] (H1)."
3. Hypothesis 2: "We further hypothesized that [H2 prediction] (H2)."
4. Competing hypotheses (if applicable): "If the [Account A] holds, [prediction A]; if [Account B] holds, [prediction B]."
5. Design preview: "[Key design feature] allowed us to [how the design tests the prediction]."
6. Pre-registration note: "All hypotheses and analysis plans were preregistered [where]."

**Full example (continued)**:

"In the present study, we used a novel Ambiguity Trust Game with 288 participants to test how trust adapts under radical uncertainty. We hypothesized that trust would be lower under ambiguity than under risk (H1). We further hypothesized that this reduction would be moderated by dispositional trust, with high-trusters maintaining trust while low-trusters withdrew, consistent with the complexity-override account (H2). By orthogonally manipulating uncertainty type (risk vs. ambiguity) and measuring dispositional trust as a continuous moderator, we directly pitted the uncertainty-avoidance and complexity-override accounts against each other. All hypotheses, exclusion criteria, and analysis plans were preregistered at [OSF link]."

## 3. Citation Integration in Introductions

### Citation Clustering Rules

**DO**: Group citations by finding, not by author
- ✅ "Trust increases under reputational incentives (Barclay, 2004; Bicchieri & Lev-On, 2007; Kosfeld et al., 2005)."
- ❌ "Barclay (2004) found that trust increases under reputational incentives. Bicchieri and Lev-On (2007) also found this. Kosfeld et al. (2005) found the same thing."

**DO**: Use author names when the specific study matters
- ✅ "Kahneman and Tversky's (1979) prospect theory challenged expected utility by showing that losses loom larger than gains."
- ❌ "Losses loom larger than gains (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)." — This finding is too specific and influential to be parenthetical.

**DO**: Cite primary sources, not secondary
- ✅ (Bandura, 1977)
- ❌ (Bandura, 1977, as cited in Smith, 2019)

**DO**: Include recent citations alongside foundational ones
- ✅ "Self-efficacy shapes persistence (Bandura, 1977; Burns et al., 2023; Chen & Williams, 2024)."
- ❌ "Self-efficacy shapes persistence (Bandura, 1977)." — This signals you haven't read anything since 1977.

### Citation Density Guidelines
- Paragraph 1 (The Field): 3-5 citations (foundational)
- Paragraph 2 (The Progress): 8-15 citations (comprehensive synthesis)
- Paragraph 3 (The Gap): 2-4 citations (specific to the gap)
- Paragraph 4 (Alternatives): 2-4 citations (theory citations)
- Paragraph 5 (This Study): 0-2 citations (methodological precedents if needed)

## 4. Gap Paragraph Construction Techniques

### The Explicit Gap Formula
"[What is unknown] + [Why it is unknown] + [What would knowing enable]"

### Gap Statement Starters (Use Exact Wording)
- "What remains unknown is whether..."
- "A critical unanswered question is whether..."
- "However, it is unclear whether..."
- "Yet no study has examined whether..."
- "The literature has not established whether..."
- "Existing evidence cannot distinguish between..."
- "A fundamental limitation of current knowledge is that..."

### Gap Construction Anti-Patterns
❌ "More research is needed." (Vague — more research is always needed.)
❌ "Few studies have examined X." (So what? Maybe X is unimportant.)
❌ "The literature on X is limited." (Limited relative to what?)
❌ "X has been understudied." (Same problem — maybe for good reason.)
❌ "Despite the importance of X, little is known." (Cliché — never use this construction.)

### Gap Strengthening Techniques
1. **Make it specific**: Not "little is known about trust" but "it is unknown whether trust under ambiguity is driven by risk aversion or heuristic processing."
2. **Show the consequence**: Not "we don't know X" but "without knowing X, we cannot design institutions that maintain cooperation during crises."
3. **Show it is tractable**: Not "X is unknown" but "X is unknown because existing paradigms fix uncertainty parameters — a limitation that a novel paradigm could overcome."
4. **Show it is timely**: Not "X is unknown" but "X is increasingly urgent as [recent event/trend] has made ambiguity the default condition of social interaction."

## 5. Broad-to-Specific Narrowing Strategies

### The Zoom Technique
Think of the introduction as a camera zoom:
1. **Wide shot (Paragraph 1)**: The entire landscape — the domain, its importance, its reach
2. **Medium shot (Paragraph 2)**: A specific region of the landscape — what has been mapped
3. **Close-up (Paragraph 3)**: The unmapped territory — the gap
4. **Microscope (Paragraph 4)**: The competing maps of this territory — alternative accounts
5. **The expedition (Paragraph 5)**: Your study — the specific journey to map this territory

### Narrowing by Variable
If your study examines Variable Z:
1. P1: The domain where Z matters
2. P2: What is known about Z and its proximal predictors (X, Y)
3. P3: What is NOT known about Z — specifically, the Z-under-[condition] question
4. P4: Competing predictions about Z under [condition]
5. P5: Your test of Z under [condition]

### Narrowing by Theory
If your study tests Theory A vs Theory B:
1. P1: The phenomenon both theories attempt to explain
2. P2: What Theory A and Theory B each explain well
3. P3: The critical test case where the theories make divergent predictions
4. P4: The specific divergence in predictions
5. P5: Your study that creates the critical test

## 6. Common Introduction Failures and Fixes

### Failure 1: The Literature Dump
**Problem**: Paragraph 2 becomes an annotated bibliography — "Smith (2019) found X. Jones (2020) found Y. Lee (2021) found Z."
**Fix**: Synthesize by finding, not by author: "Three factors influence X: [factor 1] (Smith, 2019; Lee, 2021), [factor 2] (Jones, 2020), and [factor 3] (Brown, 2022)."

### Failure 2: The Missing Gap
**Problem**: The introduction never explicitly states what is unknown. The gap is implied but never named.
**Fix**: Use the explicit gap formula. The gap should be identifiable by scanning for "unknown," "unclear," or "unanswered."

### Failure 3: The Jump Cut
**Problem**: The introduction jumps from broad claims directly to "In the present study..." without logically connecting the gap to the study.
**Fix**: Every element of the study design should be justified by a gap or alternative account mentioned earlier.

### Failure 4: The False Gap
**Problem**: The claimed gap has actually been filled — the author just didn't find or cite the existing work.
**Fix**: Search systematically. If someone has already answered the question, either narrow your gap further or explain why their answer is incomplete.

### Failure 5: The Irrelevant Field
**Problem**: Paragraph 1 opens with a broad claim that is never connected to the actual study.
**Fix**: The opening must be connected to the study through a logical chain. Every sentence must be a step toward the gap.

### Failure 6: Over-Citation in the Gap
**Problem**: The gap paragraph cites 10+ papers showing that many people have studied the area, undermining the claim that the gap exists.
**Fix**: The gap paragraph should have FEWER citations than the progress paragraph. If many people have studied this, why is the gap still there? Explain the methodological limitation.

### Failure 7: The Unmotivated Study
**Problem**: The study design appears arbitrary — why this design and not another?
**Fix**: Paragraph 5 must justify each design choice by referencing the gap or alternative accounts.

## 7. Introduction Length Calibration by Journal

| Journal Type | Word Count | Paragraphs | Citations |
|---|---|---|---|
| High-impact general (Science, Nature) | 500-700 | 3-4 (compressed) | 10-15 |
| Top field journal (JPSP, Psychological Science) | 800-1200 | 4-5 | 15-25 |
| Standard field journal | 1000-1500 | 5-6 | 20-35 |
| Review/theory journal | 1500-2500 | 6-8 | 30-50 |
| Clinical journal (JAMA, Lancet) | 400-600 | 3-4 | 8-15 |

### Compression Strategies for Short Introductions
1. Merge The Field and The Progress into one paragraph (2-3 sentences each)
2. Drop the Alternatives paragraph (embed the competing account in the Gap)
3. Reduce The Progress to 2-3 sentences (only the findings directly relevant to the gap)
4. Compress The Present Study to 2-3 sentences (hypotheses only, no design preview)

### Expansion Strategies for Long Introductions
1. Add a historical paragraph between The Field and The Progress
2. Expand The Progress into two paragraphs (one for each theme)
3. Add a theoretical framework paragraph after The Gap
4. Expand The Present Study to preview each hypothesis with its theoretical rationale

## 8. Opening Line Strategies with Examples from Top Papers

### Strategy 1: The Stakes Opening
Name what is at stake if this question is not answered.

**Example**: "Antimicrobial resistance threatens to reverse a century of medical progress." (Lancet, 2022)

**Template**: "[Phenomenon] threatens/risks/endangers [consequence of inaction]."

### Strategy 2: The Paradox Opening
State a finding that seems contradictory.

**Example**: "People cooperate more than self-interest predicts, yet cooperation routinely collapses without obvious cause." (Nature, 2018)

**Template**: "[Observation A], yet [apparent contradiction B]."

### Strategy 3: The Scale Opening
State the magnitude of a problem with a concrete number.

**Example**: "Depression affects 280 million people worldwide and is the leading cause of disability, yet only half of those treated respond to first-line therapy." (NEJM, 2021)

**Template**: "[Phenomenon] affects [number] [people/systems] and [consequence], yet [limitation]."

### Strategy 4: The Mechanism Opening
Name the mechanism that is not yet understood.

**Example**: "The neural mechanisms that transform sensory evidence into categorical decisions remain unidentified." (Science, 2019)

**Template**: "The [mechanism] that [transforms/produces/enables] [process] remains unidentified/unclear/debated."

### Strategy 5: The Challenge Opening
Directly challenge an existing assumption.

**Example**: "The assumption that memory consolidation occurs only during sleep is increasingly difficult to maintain." (Nature Neuroscience, 2020)

**Template**: "The assumption that [existing belief] is [increasingly untenable/difficult to maintain/challenged by]."

### Anti-Patterns for Opening Lines
❌ "In recent years, X has attracted considerable attention." (Says nothing.)
❌ "X plays an important role in Y." (Obvious.)
❌ "The study of X dates back to [ancient year]." (Historical trivia is not an argument.)
❌ "X is a complex phenomenon." (Everything is complex. So what?)
❌ "There is growing interest in X." (Interest is not evidence.)
