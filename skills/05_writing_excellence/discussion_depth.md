# Discussion Depth Guide

## Purpose
The discussion section is where your findings earn their meaning. It transforms statistical results into scientific contributions. This guide provides the architecture, templates, and calibration standards for discussions that are bold where evidence permits and cautious where it demands — the calibrated courage framework.

---

## 1. The Calibrated Courage Framework

### Core Principle
The discussion must be exactly as bold as the evidence warrants — no more, no less. Overclaiming triggers rejection; underclaiming triggers irrelevance. The calibrated courage framework matches claim strength to evidence strength.

### Evidence-to-Claim Calibration Table

| Evidence Strength | Permitted Claim Language | Forbidden Language |
|---|---|---|
| Preregistered RCT, replicated, large effect | "establishes," "demonstrates," "provides strong evidence" | "proves" (never appropriate in science) |
| Preregistered RCT, single study, clear effect | "shows," "indicates," "provides evidence that" | "establishes" (reserved for replication) |
| Well-powered observational, strong design | "suggests," "provides evidence consistent with" | "demonstrates" (reserved for experimental) |
| Exploratory analysis, correlational | "raises the possibility," "provides initial evidence" | "suggests" (too strong for exploratory) |
| Post-hoc, small N, unreplicated | "is consistent with," "warrants investigation" | Any definitive language |

### The Courage Audit
For every interpretive claim in the discussion, ask:
1. What is the strongest study design that produced this finding? (RCT → observational → correlational)
2. Has this finding been replicated? (Yes → no → not yet tested)
3. What is the effect size? (Large → medium → small)
4. What is the confidence interval? (Narrow → wide → includes zero)
5. Could an unmeasured confound explain this? (Unlikely → possible → plausible)

If the answer to any question weakens the evidence, downgrade the claim language by one level.

## 2. The 8-Paragraph Discussion Architecture in Detail

### PARAGRAPH 1: SUMMARY (3-4 sentences)

**Purpose**: Remind the reader what was found — not what was done, not why, not what it means. Just the findings.

**Template**:
Sentence 1: "[Study purpose] revealed [primary finding in plain language]."
Sentence 2: "Specifically, [quantified primary result]."
Sentence 3: "[Secondary finding in plain language]."
Sentence 4 (optional): "[Third finding if notable]."

**Example**:
"This study revealed that cognitive load, not awareness deficit, drives the framing effect in strategic decisions. Specifically, time pressure amplified framing-driven choice shifts by 23%, and this amplification was fully mediated by cognitive load. Furthermore, debiasing instructions that targeted awareness alone produced no reduction in the framing effect."

**Common mistakes**:
- Repeating statistics from Results (the reader just read them)
- Starting with "The results of this study..." (weak opening — lead with the finding)
- Including interpretation (save for Paragraph 2)
- Including literature comparison (save for Paragraph 5)

### PARAGRAPH 2: INTERPRETATION OF PRIMARY FINDING (5-7 sentences)

**Purpose**: Explain what the primary finding means and why it occurred, grounded in theory.

**Template**:
Sentence 1: "The primary finding that [result] is consistent with [theory/account] which posits that [mechanism]."
Sentence 2: "Under this account, [explanation of why the finding occurred]."
Sentence 3: "This contrasts with [alternative account], which would predict [opposite/null finding]."
Sentence 4: "The [magnitude/specificity] of the effect further supports [theory], because [reason]."
Sentence 5: "Previous work has [shown/suggested] [related finding], and the present results extend this by [what is new]."
Sentence 6: "[Additional theoretical connection or nuance]."
Sentence 7 (if applicable): "Notably, [unexpected aspect of the finding] suggests that [refinement of theory]."

**Example**:
"The primary finding that cognitive load fully mediates the framing amplification is consistent with dual-process theory, which posits that framing effects arise when System 2 monitoring fails and System 1 heuristics dominate (Kahneman, 2011). Under this account, time pressure disables the deliberative monitoring that would otherwise correct the framing bias, allowing the intuitive attraction to the gain-framed option to proceed unchecked. This contrasts with the awareness deficit account, which would predict that informing participants about framing should reduce the effect — a prediction not supported by our data. The complete mediation (indirect effect accounting for 89% of the total effect) further supports the cognitive load mechanism, because a partial mediation pattern would have suggested additional pathways. Previous work has shown that time pressure increases various cognitive biases (Finucane et al., 2000), and the present results extend this by identifying cognitive load as the specific mediating mechanism rather than a general depletion effect. Notably, the finding that awareness-only debiasing was ineffective suggests that merely knowing about a bias is insufficient to override it — the cognitive resources to implement the correction must also be available."

### PARAGRAPH 3: INTERPRETATION OF SECONDARY FINDING (4-6 sentences)

**Purpose**: Interpret the second key result with the same rigor as the primary finding.

**Template**: Same structure as Paragraph 2, applied to the secondary finding.

**Example**:
"The secondary finding that awareness-only debiasing failed to reduce the framing effect challenges the prevailing assumption in bias training programs that education alone can correct systematic errors (Morewedge et al., 2015). This null result is informative rather than discouraging: it narrows the search space for effective interventions by eliminating awareness as a sufficient condition. The 95% CI for the debiasing effect (d = −0.08 to 0.22) excludes effects larger than d = 0.22, suggesting that any true effect of awareness training is, at best, small. This aligns with recent meta-analytic evidence that debiasing effects are domain-specific and often fail to transfer (Sellier et al., 2019). Together with the mediation finding, this suggests a two-condition model: effective debiasing requires both awareness of the bias AND available cognitive resources to implement the correction."

### PARAGRAPH 4: INTEGRATION / MECHANISM (4-6 sentences)

**Purpose**: Show how the findings fit together into a coherent narrative. Reconcile any apparent contradictions.

**Template**:
Sentence 1: "Taken together, these findings suggest an integrated account in which [mechanism] is the primary pathway through which [phenomenon] occurs."
Sentence 2: "[How finding 1 and finding 2 combine]."
Sentence 3: "[Reconciliation of any tension between findings]."
Sentence 4: "[Broader theoretical implication of the integration]."
Sentence 5: "[How this changes the existing model]."

**Example**:
"Taken together, these findings suggest an integrated account in which cognitive load is the primary pathway through which framing distorts strategic choice. The mediation result identifies the mechanism (cognitive load reduces monitoring), while the debiasing null identifies the boundary condition (awareness without cognitive resources is insufficient). Although the null debiasing result might appear to conflict with studies showing effective debiasing (Morewedge et al., 2015), the tension resolves when considering that successful debiasing studies provided participants with both awareness AND structured decision aids that reduce cognitive demands — effectively substituting external structure for internal monitoring. This integrated account revises the standard dual-process model by adding a resource-availability gate: System 2 can override System 1 biases only when cognitive resources exceed a threshold, regardless of whether the bias is consciously recognized."

### PARAGRAPH 5: LITERATURE POSITIONING (4-6 sentences)

**Purpose**: Position this study relative to existing work. What does it add that was not known before?

**Template**:
Sentence 1: "These findings advance the literature beyond [closest prior work] by [specific contribution]."
Sentence 2: "While [Author, Year] demonstrated [related finding], the present study goes further by showing [what is new]."
Sentence 3: "This addresses the [specific limitation] noted by [Author, Year] in their discussion of [topic]."
Sentence 4: "The results also speak to the broader debate between [position A] and [position B] by [how findings inform the debate]."
Sentence 5: "In contrast to [alternative finding], which showed [different result], our findings suggest [reconciliation or reason for difference]."

**Example**:
"These findings advance the literature beyond De Martino et al. (2006), who first identified the neural correlates of framing, by identifying the mediating mechanism (cognitive load) and the boundary condition for debiasing (resource availability). While their neuroimaging work showed amygdala activation during framing, it could not determine whether this activation was the cause or consequence of the bias — our mediation analysis isolates the causal pathway. This addresses the limitation noted by Kahneman (2011) that 'we know framing exists but not precisely when and why it overwhelms rational choice.' The results also speak to the broader debate between the heuristics-and-biases program and the ecological rationality program (Gigerenzer, 2018) by showing that the framing effect is not merely a statistical artifact but reflects a genuine resource constraint that can be objectively measured and manipulated. In contrast to Treich (2019), who found that time pressure reduced framing effects in financial professionals, our findings with managers suggest the opposite — likely because professional expertise provides automatic correction routines that function as the cognitive structure our debiasing condition lacked."

### PARAGRAPH 6: LIMITATIONS (5-7 sentences)

**Purpose**: Acknowledge the boundaries of inference. For each limitation, state the direction of bias.

**Template for each limitation**:
"[Limitation description]. This may have [overestimated/underestimated] [specific effect] because [direction of bias]. [Mitigation or reason the core conclusion is likely robust despite this limitation.]"

**Required elements**:
- 2-4 limitations (not more — excessive self-criticism undermines credibility)
- Direction of bias for EACH limitation
- Mitigation argument for EACH limitation (why the core conclusion survives)
- NEVER list "small sample size" unless it truly limited power (if you found effects, N was sufficient)

**Example**:
"Several limitations qualify the interpretation of these findings. First, the sample consisted of managers from a single industry (financial services), which may limit generalizability to other professional contexts. This may have overestimated the cognitive load effect if financial professionals are more susceptible to numerical framing than the general population; however, the effect size (d = 0.63) was consistent with meta-analytic estimates from student samples (d = 0.55; Levin et al., 1998), suggesting that the effect is not driven by sample-specific factors. Second, cognitive load was operationalized through time pressure rather than a secondary task, conflating load with speed-accuracy tradeoff. This may have overestimated the specificity of the cognitive load mechanism if time pressure also induces affective stress; future research should use working memory load manipulations to isolate the cognitive component. Third, the debiasing manipulation was brief (5-minute instruction), and stronger or more sustained training might produce different results. This may have underestimated the effectiveness of awareness training, though our manipulation check confirmed that participants understood the framing concept, suggesting that awareness was achieved even if behavioral change was not. Finally, the study examined a single framing scenario, and the mechanism may differ across domains (health, legal, consumer)."

### PARAGRAPH 7: IMPLICATIONS (4-6 sentences)

**Purpose**: State what the field should do now. Be specific and actionable.

**Template**:
Sentence 1: "These findings have theoretical implications for [theory/domain]: [specific implication]."
Sentence 2: "Specifically, [what the theory should include/change/abandon]."
Sentence 3: "Practically, these results suggest that [actionable recommendation]."
Sentence 4: "[Specific intervention or design change informed by the findings]."
Sentence 5: "[Research direction — specific, not vague]."

**Example**:
"These findings have theoretical implications for dual-process models of decision-making: the models must incorporate a resource-availability gate that determines whether System 2 can override System 1, even when the bias is recognized. Specifically, the standard assumption that bias awareness enables correction should be replaced by a two-condition model requiring both awareness AND resource availability. Practically, these results suggest that debiasing interventions should shift from awareness training to structural decision aids that reduce cognitive demands — checklists, forced-consideration-of-alternatives, and pre-commitment devices that function as external monitoring. Organizations should redesign decision environments under time pressure by building in mandatory deliberation pauses rather than relying on decision-maker willpower. Future research should test whether cognitive-load-mitigating structures (e.g., decision checklists, choice architecture) effectively reduce framing bias in real organizational decisions, and whether individual differences in working memory capacity moderate susceptibility to framing under load."

### PARAGRAPH 8: CONCLUSION (1-2 sentences)

**Purpose**: Deliver the single-message takeaway. Forward-looking. Specific.

**Template**: "[Specific finding] [verb indicating contribution] by [mechanism], [forward-looking implication]."

**Calibration**:
- NOT backward-looking: "This study found that..." (reader already knows)
- NOT generic: "These findings advance our understanding of..." (vacuous)
- NOT hedged into meaninglessness: "These findings are consistent with the possibility that X might be relevant to Y in some contexts."

**Examples by strength**:

*Strong (large effect, replicated)*: "Cognitive load, not awareness deficit, is the lever through which framing distorts strategic choice — a finding that redirects debiasing from education to structural design."

*Moderate (single well-powered study)*: "Framing effects amplify under cognitive load through reduced deliberative monitoring, suggesting that debiasing requires structural support rather than awareness alone."

*Cautious (exploratory, novel domain)*: "These initial findings identify cognitive load as a candidate mechanism for framing amplification, warranting experimental tests of load-reducing interventions."

## 3. Interpretation Paragraph Template

### Structure
1. **Connect to theory**: Which theory does the finding support or challenge?
2. **Explain the mechanism**: WHY did this finding occur (theoretically)?
3. **Contrast with alternatives**: What would a competing theory have predicted?
4. **Integrate with prior work**: How does this fit with existing findings?
5. **Note refinements**: Does the finding suggest the theory needs updating?

### Verbs for Interpretation

| Evidence Strength | Interpretation Verbs |
|---|---|
| Strong | "establishes," "confirms," "demonstrates" (use sparingly) |
| Moderate | "supports," "is consistent with," "aligns with" |
| Cautious | "is compatible with," "does not contradict," "offers preliminary support for" |
| Post-hoc | "could be interpreted as," "might reflect," "one possible account is" |

## 4. Literature Positioning Techniques

### Technique 1: The Extension
"[Prior study] showed X; we extend this by showing [the new element]."
- Use when: Your finding builds directly on prior work
- Example: "De Martino et al. (2006) identified the neural signature of framing; we extend this by identifying the mediating cognitive mechanism."

### Technique 2: The Reconciliation
"[Study A] found X; [Study B] found Y. Our findings reconcile this by showing [unifying principle]."
- Use when: The literature contains apparently contradictory findings
- Example: "While time pressure reduced framing in Treich (2019) but increased it in our study, this reconciliation emerges when considering expertise: professionals possess automatic correction routines that function under pressure, whereas non-experts do not."

### Technique 3: The Challenge
"Our findings challenge [existing assumption/theory] by showing [contradictory evidence]."
- Use when: Your results directly contradict a prevailing view
- Calibrate: Only use "challenge" when the evidence is strong (preregistered, adequately powered, clear effect)
- Example: "These findings challenge the assumption that bias awareness enables correction, showing instead that awareness without cognitive resources produces no behavioral change."

### Technique 4: The Boundary
"[Prior finding] holds under [condition]; our results identify the boundary at [new condition]."
- Use when: Your study identifies when a known effect does or does not occur
- Example: "The established effect of social exclusion on aggression holds under controlled conditions but may not extend to situations where cognitive resources are available for emotion regulation."

## 5. Limitations Paragraph Strategy

### The Direction-of-Bias Method

Every limitation must state which direction it biases the results. This transforms a weakness into a precise qualification.

| Limitation | Direction of Bias | Effect on Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Convenience sample (WEIRD) | May overestimate effect if WEIRD participants are more susceptible | Generalizability is limited; effect likely present but possibly smaller in diverse samples |
| Single operationalization of DV | May underestimate effect if measure has low reliability | True effect may be larger than estimated |
| Self-report measures | May overestimate effect due to common method variance | Effect may partly reflect shared method rather than construct relationship |
| Cross-sectional design | Direction of causation unclear; may overestimate causal effect | Cannot establish temporal precedence; experimental replication needed |
| Short-term measurement | May overestimate effect if effects decay | Long-term effects unknown; durability needs testing |
| Lack of active control | May overestimate specific treatment effect | Non-specific factors (attention, expectancy) not ruled out |
| Multiple comparisons without correction | May overestimate number of true effects | Individual findings may not replicate; correction applied in sensitivity analysis |
| Researcher-designed manipulation | May overestimate effect due to demand characteristics | Ecological validity may be limited; field replication needed |

### The Mitigation Structure
For each limitation: [Limitation] → [Direction of bias] → [Why core conclusion survives]

**Template**: "A limitation is [description]. This may have [over/under]estimated [specific effect] because [mechanism of bias]. However, [mitigating evidence: effect consistency with other methods, manipulation check, convergence with prior findings, or sensitivity analysis result], suggesting that the core conclusion is [robust/qualified] despite this limitation."

### Limitations Anti-Patterns
❌ "A limitation is the small sample size." (If you found significant effects, sample size was sufficient for detection.)
❌ "Future research should replicate these findings." (This is always true and thus meaningless.)
❌ "Our study has several limitations." (Of course it does — name them specifically.)
❌ Listing 7+ limitations (undermines credibility; identify the 2-4 most important)
❌ Stating limitations without direction of bias (leaves the reader uncertain how to weight the finding)

## 6. Implications Paragraph Template

### Theoretical Implications
1. **What the theory must add**: "These findings suggest that [theory] must incorporate [new element]."
2. **What the theory must change**: "The standard assumption that [X] must be revised to account for [finding]."
3. **What the theory can abandon**: "The finding that [null result] undermines the theoretical claim that [assumption]."

### Practical Implications
1. **For practitioners**: "Practitioners should [specific action] rather than [current practice]."
2. **For policymakers**: "Policymakers should [specific policy change] based on [specific finding]."
3. **For designers**: "System designers should [specific design change] to prevent [specific failure]."

### Future Research (Specific, Not Vague)
❌ "Future research should explore this further." (Vague)
❌ "More studies are needed." (Always true)
✅ "Future research should test whether working memory training reduces framing susceptibility by increasing available cognitive resources for bias monitoring."
✅ "Longitudinal studies tracking debiasing effects over 6-month periods would determine whether structural interventions produce lasting change or whether cognitive adaptation eventually bypasses the structure."

## 8. Common Discussion Failures

### Failure 1: Overclaiming
**Problem**: The evidence supports a correlation; the discussion claims causation.
**Fix**: Match claim language to design. Observational data → "associated with," not "caused." Experimental data with randomization → "caused" or "produced."

### Failure 2: Underclaiming
**Problem**: A well-powered RCT with a large effect is described as "providing preliminary evidence consistent with the possibility that..."
**Fix**: Upgrade claim language. Strong evidence warrants strong language. Triple-hedging undermines contribution.

### Failure 3: Circular Reasoning
**Problem**: "The results support the theory because the theory predicts the results, and the results confirm the theory."
**Fix**: Explain the mechanism — WHY does the theory predict this? What is the causal chain? A discussion that merely restates "consistent with theory" without explanation adds nothing.

### Failure 4: The Re-Results Section
**Problem**: The discussion repeats statistics from the results section.
**Fix**: The discussion reports findings in plain language without statistics. The reader has already seen the numbers; now they need the meaning.

### Failure 5: The Literature Review Revisited
**Problem**: The discussion introduces entirely new literature not mentioned in the introduction.
**Fix**: New citations in the discussion should CONNECT to the introduction's literature, not open entirely new domains. Exception: genuinely surprising findings may warrant brief connection to unexpected literatures.

### Failure 6: The Non-Limitations Section
**Problem**: Limitations that are not really limitations: "A limitation is that our sample was drawn from only one country" — when the effect is a basic cognitive process unlikely to vary by culture.
**Fix**: Only list limitations that could plausibly change the interpretation. If the limitation is unlikely to affect the conclusion, it is not worth mentioning.

### Failure 7: The Vague Future Directions
**Problem**: "Future research should investigate this phenomenon further." (Investigate what? How? Why?)
**Fix**: Specify the exact question, the recommended design, and the theoretical motivation: "Future research should test whether working memory training reduces framing effects using a pre-post design that measures both WM capacity and framing susceptibility, which would determine whether the cognitive load mechanism is malleable."

### Failure 8: The Weak Conclusion
**Problem**: The final sentence is "These findings contribute to the growing literature on X." (So what?)
**Fix**: The conclusion must be specific, forward-looking, and memorable. It should be the sentence a reader remembers after putting the paper down. Use the single-message principle: one idea, clearly stated.
