# Nature Family Journal Format Template

> **CRES Template — Nature Family Style**
> Covers Nature, Nature Medicine, Nature Neuroscience, Nature Biotechnology, Nature Methods, Nature Physics, Nature Chemistry, and all Nature Research journals.

---

## 1. Overview

Nature Research journals are among the most selective and impactful scientific publications. Their formatting requirements are distinctive: no numbered sections in the main text, strict word limits, a separate Methods section at the end, and very specific figure/table conventions. Nature's editorial philosophy prioritizes clarity for a broad scientific audience, demanding accessible writing free of specialized jargon.

## 2. Article Types & Word Limits

| Article Type           | Main Text | Methods | Abstract | Figures/Tables |
|------------------------|-----------|---------|----------|----------------|
| Article (Nature)       | 2,500     | 3,000   | 150      | 4–6 display items |
| Letter (Nature)        | 1,500     | 2,000   | 150      | 3–4 display items |
| Article (Nature Med.)  | 3,500     | 3,000   | 150      | 6–8 display items |
| Article (Nature Neuro.)| 3,000     | 2,500   | 150      | 5–7 display items |
| Brief Communication    | 1,200     | 800     | 70       | 2–3 display items |
| Resource               | 3,500     | 3,000   | 150      | 6–8 display items |

**Display items** = figures + tables combined. Supplementary Information has no page limit.

## 3. Title & Author Format

### Title
- **Maximum 90 characters** (including spaces) for Nature
- **Maximum 120 characters** for most Nature sub-journals
- No abbreviations (except standard: DNA, RNA, HIV, etc.)
- Active, declarative style preferred
- Must be understandable to scientists in other disciplines
- Avoid questions as titles
- ❌ Bad: "A study on the role of p53 in DNA damage response"
- ✅ Good: "p53 orchestrates DNA damage repair through BRCA1 recruitment"

### Author List
```
John A. Smith¹², Jane B. Doe³, Robert C. Lee¹, Maria Garcia⁴ &
William T. Johnson¹²*

¹Department of Molecular Biology, University of Cambridge, Cambridge, UK.
²Cambridge Institute for Therapeutic Immunology & Biotechnology,
  University of Cambridge, Cambridge, UK.
³Division of Oncology, Stanford University School of Medicine,
  Stanford, CA, USA.
⁴Institute of Cancer Research, Barcelona, Spain.
*e-mail: w.johnson@cam.ac.uk
```

### Author List Rules
- Use initials for first/middle names (J. A. Smith)
- Superscript numbers for affiliations
- Multiple affiliations: superscript 1,2 (comma-separated)
- Corresponding author marked with asterisk (*)
- "et al." used if >5 authors on first mention in text
- Provide ORCID IDs (strongly encouraged)
- Provide Twitter/X handles (optional)

### Affiliation Format
- Department, Institution, City, State/Province, Country
- No street addresses or zip codes
- Use official institution names in English
- Group multiple affiliations under the same institution

## 4. Abstract Requirements

Nature's abstract requirements are among the most restrictive in scientific publishing:

### Rules
- **Maximum 150 words** (strictly enforced)
- **No citations** whatsoever
- **No specialized terminology** without explanation
- **No abbreviations** unless universally understood (DNA, RNA)
- Must be a single paragraph
- Must be comprehensible to a scientist in any discipline
- Written in the third person (avoid "we show," prefer passive construction)

### Abstract Structure (Implicit)
1. **Context** (1–2 sentences): What is the general area and why does it matter?
2. **Problem** (1 sentence): What specific gap or challenge exists?
3. **Approach** (1–2 sentences): What did you do?
4. **Key Result** (1–2 sentences): What is the main finding?
5. **Significance** (1 sentence): Why does this matter broadly?

### Abstract Template
```
[General context statement]. [Specific problem or gap]. Here we show
[main finding], using [approach/method]. [Key quantitative result].
[Second key result]. [Broader significance and implications]. These
findings [impact statement].
```

### ❌ Common Abstract Mistakes
- Exceeding 150 words
- Including citations like³
- Using field-specific jargon ("allosteric modulator," "epigenetic reader")
- Being vague about results ("we found significant improvements")
- Writing in first person ("We discovered that...")
- Including methodology details better suited to Methods

## 5. Main Text Structure

### Critical Rule: NO Numbered Sections in Main Text

Nature main text uses **bold paragraph leads** instead of numbered section headings. The text flows as continuous prose with thematic paragraphs introduced by bold text.

### Main Text Organization

```
[Title]
[Authors & Affiliations]
[Abstract]

[Lead paragraph — bold, no heading — serves as Introduction]

[Body text organized by logical flow, not numbered sections]

[Paragraph leads in bold indicate thematic transitions]
[Bold paragraph leads replace section headings]

[Last paragraph serves as Discussion/conclusion]

---
[Methods section — separated by horizontal rule — numbered if needed]
[References]
[Acknowledgments]
[Author Contributions]
[Competing Interests]
[Data Availability]
[Code Availability]
```

### Lead Paragraph (Introduction)
- Written in **bold** at the start of the main text
- Serves as the Introduction
- 1 paragraph, typically 150–200 words
- Must convey: broad context → specific problem → gap → what this paper does
- Written for a general scientific audience (Nature readership spans all disciplines)
- Must NOT assume field-specific knowledge

### Body Paragraphs
- No numbered sections; use **bold paragraph leads** for thematic transitions
- Bold leads: a few words at the start of a paragraph, set in bold, summarizing the paragraph's point
- Example: "**BRCA1 recruitment is essential for repair.** We next examined whether..."
- Results and interpretation are interwoven (no separate Results/Discussion in Nature main text)
- Typically 2–3 pages of main text for an Article

### Final Paragraph
- Serves as the Discussion/Conclusion
- Placed at the end of main text
- Provides broader significance, implications, and future directions
- Should NOT repeat results verbatim
- May include caveats and limitations

### Transition Words & Phrases
- Use Nature-style transitions between paragraphs:
  - "To investigate this, we..."
  - "We next asked whether..."
  - "Given these findings, we examined..."
  - "Consistent with this model, ..."
  - "In contrast to previous reports, ..."
  - "These results suggest that..."

## 6. Methods Section

The Methods section is **separated from the main text** by a horizontal rule and appears **before the references**.

### Key Requirements
- Can use numbered subsections (unlike main text)
- Maximum 3,000 words for Article (varies by journal)
- Must be detailed enough for reproducibility
- Include: sample sizes, statistical tests, equipment models, reagent catalog numbers
- Ethics statements required for human/animal work

### Methods Subsection Template
```
Methods

Cell culture. HEK293T cells (ATCC CRL-3216) were maintained in DMEM
(Gibco 11965092) supplemented with 10% FBS (Gibco 26140079) and 1%
penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco 15140122) at 37 °C with 5% CO₂.
Cells were authenticated by STR profiling and tested negative for
mycoplasma contamination.

CRISPR-Cas9 knockout. Guide RNAs were designed using CRISPick
(Broad Institute) and cloned into lentiCRISPRv2 (Addgene #52961).
Lentivirus was produced in HEK293T cells using psPAX2 and pMD2.G
packaging plasmids. Target cells were transduced at MOI 0.3 and
selected with puromycin (2 μg/ml) for 7 days.

Statistical analysis. All experiments were performed with at least
three biological replicates. Statistical significance was assessed
using two-tailed Student's t-test for two-group comparisons or
one-way ANOVA with Tukey's post-hoc test for multiple comparisons.
P values < 0.05 were considered significant. Effect sizes (Cohen's d)
and 95% confidence intervals are reported. No statistical methods
were used to predetermine sample size.
```

### Methods Must Include
- Sample size justification
- Inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Randomization procedures
- Blinding protocols
- Statistical methods with assumptions
- Software versions and parameters
- Antibody catalog numbers and dilutions
- Cell line authentication and mycoplasma testing
- Ethics committee approval numbers

## 7. Figure Formatting

### Figure Requirements
- **Initial submission:** Single PDF with all figures (one per page)
- **Final submission:** Individual high-resolution files
- **Minimum resolution:** 300 dpi at final print size
- **Color mode:** RGB for online, CMYK for print
- **File formats:** TIFF, EPS, or high-quality PDF
- **Maximum figure width:** 183 mm (single column) or 247 mm (1.5 column)
- **Figure height:** Maximum 260 mm

### Figure Types & Sizes
| Type          | Width         | Description                          |
|---------------|---------------|--------------------------------------|
| Single column | 89 mm         | Standard size                        |
| 1.5 column   | 120 mm        | Intermediate size                    |
| Double column | 183 mm        | Full-width figure                    |

### Figure Layout
- Multi-panel figures: label panels (a, b, c, d) in lowercase bold
- Panel labels: 8 pt Helvetica bold, top-left of each panel
- Use consistent font sizes across all panels
- Axis labels: 6–8 pt
- Scale bars: include in figure, not in caption
- White space between panels: 2–4 mm
- Background: white (not gray or colored)

### Figure Caption Format
```
Figure 1 | BRCA1 recruitment to DNA damage sites is p53-dependent.
a, Representative immunofluorescence images of γH2AX foci (green) and
BRCA1 (red) in control and p53-knockout cells. Scale bar, 10 μm.
b, Quantification of BRCA1 foci per nucleus (n = 150 cells per condition
from three independent experiments). Box plots show median, interquartile
range, and whiskers extend to 1.5× IQR. **P < 0.01, two-tailed t-test.
c, Western blot analysis of BRCA1 protein levels. d, Model of p53-mediated
BRCA1 recruitment to DNA damage sites.
```

### Figure Caption Rules
- Title after "Figure X |" (pipe character, not colon or period)
- Title is a **complete sentence** describing the main point
- Panel descriptions start with lowercase letter and comma: "a, Description..."
- Define all symbols, colors, and abbreviations in caption
- Include sample sizes and statistical tests
- Include scale bars and define units
- "n" always defined (biological vs. technical replicates)

### Color Guidelines
- Ensure figures work in grayscale
- Use colorblind-friendly palettes (viridis, okabe-ito)
- Avoid red-green combinations
- Nature provides specific color palettes for consistency

## 8. Table Formatting

### Table Requirements
- Tables are display items counted toward the limit
- Simple, clean format with minimal horizontal rules
- No vertical rules
- Title above the table
- Footnotes below using superscript letters (not numbers or symbols)

### Table Template
```
Table 1 | Clinical characteristics of study participants

Characteristic         Control (n=50)    Treatment (n=52)    P value
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Age, years             54.2 ± 8.3        52.7 ± 9.1         0.39
Male sex               28 (56%)          30 (58%)           0.84
BMI, kg/m²            27.1 ± 4.2        26.8 ± 3.9         0.71
Tumor stage
  I–II                 22 (44%)          24 (46%)           0.82
  III–IV              28 (56%)          28 (54%)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Values are mean ± s.d. or n (%). P values from two-tailed
t-test or chi-squared test.
```

## 9. Reference Format (Nature Style)

### In-Text Citation
- Superscript numbers: "as shown previously³"
- Multiple citations: ³,⁴ or ³–⁵ (consecutive)
- Place citation numbers AFTER punctuation: "results were significant.³"
- Do NOT include author names with citations in main text

### Reference List Format

```
1. Smith, J. A., Doe, J. B. & Lee, R. C. p53 orchestrates DNA
   damage repair through BRCA1 recruitment. Nature 610, 123–129
   (2024).

2. Wang, L. et al. A survey on foundation models for scientific
   discovery. Nat. Methods 21, 456–468 (2024).

3. Johnson, W. T. Deep Learning in Biomedicine (Cambridge Univ.
   Press, 2023).

4. Garcia, M. Oncogene regulation in breast cancer. PhD thesis,
   Univ. Barcelona (2022).

5. NIH. Cancer Statistics 2024. https://www.cancer.gov/statistics
   (2024).
```

### Key Reference Rules
- List all authors up to 5; use "et al." for 6+
- Author initials BEFORE surnames: J. A. Smith (not Smith, J. A.)
- Journal name italicized and abbreviated (use standard abbreviations)
- Volume number in bold: **610**
- Page range without "pp.": 123–129
- Year in parentheses: (2024)
- No article titles in references for some Nature sub-journals (check specific journal)

## 10. Extended Data & Supplementary Information

### Extended Data
- Peer-reviewed figures and tables that are essential but cannot fit in print
- Labeled "Extended Data Fig. 1," "Extended Data Table 1," etc.
- Appear online only (not in print)
- Must be cited in the main text
- Same quality standards as main figures
- Extended Data captions compiled in a separate list after the main reference list

### Supplementary Information
- Non-essential supporting material
- NOT peer-reviewed (since 2024 Nature policy change)
- Includes: raw data, additional analyses, large tables, code
- Submitted as a single PDF
- Must be cited in main text as "Supplementary Information" or "Supplementary Table 1"
- No specific formatting requirements

### Supplementary Note Structure
```
Supplementary Note 1: Detailed Derivation of the Statistical Model
Supplementary Note 2: Convergence Analysis

Supplementary Table 1: Complete list of identified mutations
Supplementary Table 2: Raw data for all experiments

Supplementary Figure 1: Additional validation experiments
Supplementary Figure 2: Sensitivity analysis

Supplementary Methods: Additional experimental details
Supplementary References: Separate reference list for SI
```

## 11. Author Contribution Statement (CRediT)

Nature uses the CRediT (Contributor Roles Taxonomy) system:

```
Author contributions: J.A.S. conceived the project and wrote the
manuscript. J.A.S. and J.B.D. designed experiments. J.B.D. and R.C.L.
performed experiments. M.G. analyzed data and developed computational
tools. W.T.J. supervised the project and secured funding. All authors
discussed results and commented on the manuscript.
```

### CRediT Role Categories
- Conceptualization
- Methodology
- Software
- Validation
- Formal analysis
- Investigation
- Resources
- Data curation
- Writing – original draft
- Writing – review & editing
- Visualization
- Supervision
- Project administration
- Funding acquisition

## 12. Reporting Summary Requirements

Every Nature paper must include a **Life Sciences Reporting Summary** (for biology/medicine) or equivalent:

```
Reporting Summary

Nature Research wishes to improve the reproducibility of the work
published in Nature. This Reporting Summary provides linked information
about the experimental design, and characteristics of the subjects/materials
used in the study.

Sample size: Determined by power analysis (α = 0.05, power = 0.80)
Replication: All key findings replicated in ≥3 independent experiments
Randomization: Mice randomly assigned to treatment groups using
  computer-generated random numbers
Blinding: Investigators blinded to group assignment during data
  collection and analysis
Statistical methods: Two-tailed t-test, one-way ANOVA with Tukey's
  correction, log-rank test for survival analysis
```

## 13. Common Nature Formatting Mistakes

| # | Mistake | Correction |
|---|---------|-----------|
| 1 | Using numbered sections in main text | Use bold paragraph leads instead |
| 2 | Abstract over 150 words | Strictly enforced; trim to ≤150 |
| 3 | Jargon in abstract | Must be accessible to all scientists |
| 4 | First person in abstract | Use passive/third person |
| 5 | Too many display items | Stay within the limit for article type |
| 6 | Methods integrated with results | Must be separate, at the end |
| 7 | Missing scale bars in microscopy | Always include scale bars in figures |
| 8 | Not defining "n" | Specify biological vs. technical replicates |
| 9 | Red-green color scheme | Use colorblind-friendly palettes |
| 10 | Panel labels uppercase "A, B" | Use lowercase "a, b" |
| 11 | Colon in figure caption title | Use pipe: "Figure 1 \| Title" |
| 12 | Missing statistical details | Report exact P values, effect sizes, CI |
| 13 | Not separating Extended Data from SI | Extended Data is peer-reviewed; SI is not |
| 14 | Missing CRediT contributions | Required for all Nature papers |
| 15 | Title over 90 characters | Keep ≤90 characters for Nature |

## 14. Submission Checklist

### Pre-Submission Content
- [ ] Main text within word limit (2,500 for Nature Article)
- [ ] Abstract ≤150 words, no citations, no jargon
- [ ] Title ≤90 characters
- [ ] Display items within limit (4–6 for Nature Article)
- [ ] Methods section separate, detailed, ≤3,000 words
- [ ] All statistical tests reported with exact P values
- [ ] Sample sizes justified
- [ ] CRediT author contributions written
- [ ] Competing interests declared for all authors

### Figure Quality
- [ ] All figures ≥300 dpi at print size
- [ ] Panel labels lowercase bold (a, b, c)
- [ ] Scale bars included in microscopy images
- [ ] Colorblind-friendly palettes used
- [ ] Figure captions use pipe format: "Figure 1 | Title"
- [ ] All symbols and colors defined in captions
- [ ] Sample sizes (n) reported in captions

### Extended Data & SI
- [ ] Extended Data figures/tables cited in main text
- [ ] Extended Data captions compiled separately
- [ ] Supplementary Information cited in main text
- [ ] Source data files prepared for figures

### Compliance
- [ ] Ethics approval documented (IRB/IACUC numbers)
- [ ] Informed consent obtained (human subjects)
- [ ] Data availability statement included
- [ ] Code availability statement included
- [ ] Reporting summary completed
- [ ] ARRIVE guidelines followed (animal research)
- [ ] CONSORT guidelines followed (clinical trials)

### Final
- [ ] Cover letter written (emphasize broad significance)
- [ ] Suggested reviewers identified (5–10, no conflicts)
- [ ] Opposed reviewers listed (with justification)
- [ ] Presubmission inquiry sent (recommended for Nature)
- [ ] All authors approved the manuscript
- [ ] Submitted via Nature's online system

## 15. Nature Sub-Journal Specific Notes

### Nature Medicine
- Focus on translational/clinical relevance
- Requires clinical trial registration numbers
- Demands patient cohort characterization
- ICMJE conflict of interest forms required

### Nature Neuroscience
- Requires detailed electrophysiology parameters
- Brain atlas coordinates for imaging
- Animal welfare documentation

### Nature Biotechnology
- Methods must include detailed protocols
- Software tools must be publicly available
- Patent/IP status should be disclosed

### Nature Methods
- Detailed protocol descriptions essential
- Comparison with existing methods required
- Resource availability is paramount
- Benchmarking on standard datasets expected

### Nature Physics
- Theoretical predictions must have experimental validation
- Error analysis must be comprehensive
- Units must follow SI conventions
